New manhole covers make mark in Madison
JAY RATH
For the State Journal
Contact Jay Rath through daybreak@madison.com.
New works of public art are slowly being introduced around the city. The large iron medallions feature a bas relief of the Capitol dome dividing night and daytime skies. And while so many recent works of public art have been in-your-face controversial, these pieces are beaux arts beautiful, pleasant and shyly underfoot. Literally.

They are our new manhole lids.

Manhole lids - officially termed "sewer access covers" - were identified as an art form of sorts in 1994, with the publication of "Designs Underfoot: The Art of Manhole Covers in New York City."

Art or not, "They really are a good-looking lid, aren't they?" asks Kathy Cryan, operations manager of Madison's Engineering Division.

No one is more excited about the manhole lids than Mark Schmitz, who, with Tina Remy and their ZD Studios, designed them.

"We had talked about this quite some time ago," says Schmitz. "I was aware it was out there as a possibility, but I didn't know until just now. Is it done? Is it on a manhole cover somewhere? Where is one?"

The confusion comes from the image's history.

"The design was to have been incorporated into the Cottage Grove (Road) bridge over Interstate 39," says Larry Nelson, city engineer. "However, that bridge was not reconstructed but retrofitted for bikes and pedestrians. We had considered a sewer access cover with a city engineering designation, and Kathy Cryan worked with the foundry to produce them, using the logo from the bridge."

The covers are produced in sets, along with the large metal collars on which they sit. A lid is 23 inches in diameter, 1 inches thick and 90 pounds of cast iron. A set costs either $178 or $210 depending on the depth of the collar.

"But these things are going to last 50, 75 even 100 years," notes Cryan. "They're very durable."

They're made by the Neenah Foundry, and the custom sets cost $10 more than the no-frills versions. The city bought 175 and started placing them in May. You'll find them in new subdivisions and, Downtown, in the repaved bus lane on University Avenue, west of Park Street.

"And as we need to replace a manhole lid, we'll be using these," says Cryan. "We have well in excess of 16,000 structures out there with lids on them. We're not looking at any wholesale changeover. Just as something becomes worn, this will be the new one that goes on."

As for the design, "I was the creative director and kind of did all the inspirational sketches that were then passed to my staff for development," says Schmitz, of ZD Studios, 111 S. Hamilton St. The firm also contributed to the recent, ongoing State Street redesign project. The manhole design was to be cast in cement.

"I knew what I wanted to do. I knew I wanted to have a nighttime sky along with a daytime sky," says Schmitz. "I wanted to show the Capitol with the city of Madison as being a beautiful nighttime city and a beautiful daytime city."

He says he's "very proud of creating a wonderful front door to Madison's sewer access." He laughs as he says it but earnestly adds, "Our city works are what make our city work. Being a native to this city, I will be proud to share with my grandchildren the accomplishments of their grandfather's design firm. We're always pleased when we can create public art, as it were, for the city."

And Cryan notes that the new covers are practical, too. "What's nice about it, from an operational standpoint, as they get changed over it's easy for someone to identify a city of Madison lid and structure from an MG&E, from an AT&T, from whatever fiber company, from water, from Madison Metro Sewage District," she says. "At some intersections, particularly in the Downtown area, it's like, 'Wow! Look at all these lids!' It makes it easy for people to know who to contact if there's a problem with a structure. That was our real motivation."

But Cryan also can't help playing art critic.

"It's bold and strong," she says. "We actually have a manhole in our training room. The guys wire-brushed it down and sprayed it metallic silver. It looks really cool!"
